# How Decorative Crosswalks Build Community Identity

A crosswalk is infrastructure. Its job is to get pedestrians safely from one side of a street to the other. But in the right hands — and with the right materials — a crosswalk becomes something more: a statement about who a community is, what it values, and how it chooses to be seen.

## Placemaking at Street Level

Urban planners have long understood the concept of placemaking — designing public spaces that foster social interaction, local identity, and civic pride. Most placemaking interventions focus on parks, plazas, and building facades. But streets make up 30–40% of urban land area in most Canadian cities. Crosswalks sit at the most socially active intersections of that network.

Decorative crosswalk treatments — from Pride rainbow crossings to Indigenous art installations to neighbourhood heritage patterns — have emerged as one of the most visible and emotionally resonant placemaking tools available to municipalities.

## Vancouver's Model

The City of Vancouver has been a national leader in decorative crosswalk programming. Through initiatives like "More Awesome Now" and the Vancouver Mural Festival, the city has used StreetBond coloured pavement treatments and custom StreetPrint stamping to transform intersections in Commercial Drive, Granville Island, and the Downtown Eastside.

The results are measurable: businesses in corridors with decorative surface treatments report increased foot traffic and dwell time. Neighbourhood associations report increased community pride. And the treatments generate significant social media engagement — turning everyday intersections into destinations.

## Indigenous Recognition

One of the most meaningful applications of decorative crosswalk technology is in reconciliation and Indigenous recognition. HUB Surface Systems has worked with multiple municipalities and First Nations to create crosswalk installations featuring traditional art motifs, territorial acknowledgements, and cultural symbols.

The UBC Musqueam crosswalk — created in collaboration with the Musqueam Nation — features traditional Coast Salish weaving patterns translated into stamped asphalt. It is simultaneously functional infrastructure, public art, and a visible statement of the university's commitment to reconciliation.
